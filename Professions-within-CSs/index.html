Welcome to the Scope of Practice in SLP and Audiology

This interactive experience introduces you to the core roles and responsibilities of speech-language pathologists and audiologists, based on the ASHA Scope of Practice (2016).

Prevention

Speech-language pathologists engage in prevention and wellness activities aimed at reducing the incidence of communication disorders. They promote public awareness, educate families, and implement strategies that reduce risks across the lifespan.

Assessment

Assessment is a comprehensive, collaborative process that identifies the nature and extent of communication and swallowing disorders. SLPs use observation, tools, and cultural sensitivity to create accurate, individualized profiles.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is the outcome of assessment. It involves identifying disorders, determining severity, and making recommendations. SLPs use this step to justify interventions, collaborate with stakeholders, and ensure evidence-based decisions.

Treatment

Treatment is individualized, goal-driven, and dynamic. SLPs develop plans that improve function, participation, and quality of life. Services may include counseling, technology use, AAC, therapy, or education, always grounded in evidence.

Speech-Language Pathologists

SLPs work across ages and settings, serving individuals with speech, language, literacy, voice, fluency, and swallowing disorders. Their scope is defined by ethics, continuing education, and collaboration with families and teams.

Audiologists

Audiologists assess and manage hearing and balance. Their services include diagnosis, hearing aid fitting, counseling, prevention of noise-induced hearing loss, and working with SLPs on shared outcomes.

ASHA

The American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) guides the professions of SLP and audiology. It sets certification standards, promotes research, provides ethical codes, and advocates for high-quality care nationwide.
